Ahmad's Book Recommendations

The Open Society and Its Enemies by Karl Popper

When Erik Kandel went on a limb to study memory by analyzing neural processes in the giant snail Aplysia, Karl Popper encouraged him. But he didn't encourage Kandel by telling him that he was going to be right. The nature of Popper's encouragement captures his attitude towards science.

Aplysia has few neurons, and they are large, making its nervous system easy to study. When Kandel was researching them, almost all of his colleagues believed that you could not extrapolate learnings from non-complex systems, like giant snails, to complex ones, like humans. Kandel thought that it might be possible, and he decided to go against the grain.

Popper told Kandel that he should try and run head first into the face of failure. In Popper's view, Kandel's job as a scientist was not to be right. His job was to make sure that beliefs were grounded in evidence, and there was limited evidence that non-complex systems could not inform the study of complex ones. Kandel ran towards failure, and as luck would have it, there was no failure on the other end. There was a Nobel prize. (Read In Search of Memory by Erik Kandel to learn more about Kandel's work and his recollection of his interactions with Popper.)

In The Open Society, Popper carefully dismantles political ideas that make us more inclined to believe in unproven positives rather than acknowledging the true limitations of our knowledge. It's a remarkable and inspiring demonstration of Popper's own analytical ability, which allowed him to see past centuries old biases and outline a concise attack on long-standing traditions of political philosophy.

Flights by Olga Tokarczuk

In Black Boy, Richard Wright talks about how he could not write well when he was younger. He tried, but nothing came of it. So he resorted to writing perfect sentences. Great sentences that would transport him to a different world.

No small amount of credit goes to Jennifer Croft for what is truly a remarkable English translation of Tokarczuk's Polish text. But one can only marvel at the perfection with which Tokarczuk executes each sentence, and strings them together to write passages that provoke flights of thought.

My favorite story is that of a mother who leaves home one day and doesn't return for a long time. She remains homeless in a train station. But it isn't a story about someone escaping from the normalcy of their life. It is a story about what happened, with no why or hows behind it. It's a story that provokes wonder instead of analysis. And that is Tokarzcuk's magic. To distance her work from frameworks of analysis and let things be.

Perhaps Tokarzcuk lets us know what to expect in the beginning. She talks about her days as a student of psychology and her fascination with what is odd instead of what is ordinary. One thing is for sure, there is nothing ordinary about Flights.

Super-infinite by Katherine Rundell

Donne was an English poet, a contemporary of Shakespeare. At one point in his life, he almost died as a prisoner in the Tower of London. At another, he was one of the most loved preachers in London, so much so that the King attended his sermons. Donne's poetry wouldn't be published until after sdfhis death. But his remarkable style and skill would quickly establish him as a master innovator.

Donne's biography, like all biographies, is as much a story as it is fact. After all, it is only through curation that a whole life can fit into a book. And as truthful as Rundell's biography probably is, she leaves no leaf unturned to show how uncertain and improbable life can be. And she carries out her work of non-fiction with the kind of rare flair with which Virginia Woolf wrote the fictional biography of Orlando, another story about a highly improbable life.

It is refreshing to read a recounting of someone's life that does not search for reasons behind their greatness. And instead focuses on the fragile moments in which luck and chance, more than anything, led to the creation of a fascinating personage.

Maybe Rundell writes in the same spirit as Donne's works. In her work, Donne comes off as uniquely human, but that adds to the the marvels he wrote during his life. And Donne's own works, while striving for the spirit of infinity, are grounded in our messy, non-ideal, physical world.

Being and Time by Martin Heidegger

Some books, like math theory textbooks, require careful study. And even then, much remains in the way of mastery. Being and Time falls squarely into that category, partly due its complexity, and partly due to the fact that Heidegger did not care to write well. It's not a text that I would have read outside of a course focused only on it with a Heidegger expert, Professor Martin Hagglund. But it is a text well worth reading.

Heidegger is interested in the most basic philosophical question: How do we experience everyday life as humans? And he doesn't take an experimental approach. He uses formal logic to dissect the very nature of everyday human experience. But his deep, philosophical analysis reminds one of experimental and theoretical frameworks, like Daniel Kahneman's System 1 and System 2 (fast and slow thinking), or Piaget's theory of child development.

Though, ultimately, Heidegger's book is trying to communicate the central importance of finiteness. He posits that any action we take in life has meaning only because our lives are finite. Taking any action always comes at the cost of time, and there is no way to recover that cost.

But Heidegger is not asking us to focus our energies on good, important actions. Not in the slightest. He coolly accepts that we have very little control over the circumstances in which are born and in which we live our lives. Instead, his book on being offers us an orthogonal choice: to care or not care about what we're doing.
